Overview

This study evaluates the aerodynamic performance of a multi-element rear wing using CFD simulations in STAR-CCM+. The analysis focuses on lift coefficient (Cl), drag coefficient (Cd), and aerodynamic efficiency (|Cl|/Cd) across varying angles of attack.

Results show that downforce (negative Cl) increases with angle until approximately 40–45 degrees, after which the curve flattens, indicating stall onset. Efficiency is highest at low angles (0–5°) and decreases as drag rises significantly at higher angles.

The results demonstrate the trade-off between downforce and drag, with an optimal operating range around 10–20 degrees for balanced performance. This behaviour is consistent with multi-element aerodynamic systems, where slot flow delays separation, leading to gradual stall characteristics.

Slot flow delays separation — yielding gradual, predictable stall.

Higher angles produce maximum downforce but with substantial drag penalties. The multi-element slot energises the boundary layer, pushing stall onset beyond what a single-element profile would achieve.

Conclusion

The CFD analysis confirms that the multi-element rear wing performs within expected aerodynamic boundaries. The 10–20° angle-of-attack window provides the best compromise between downforce generation and drag penalty.

Slot-flow effects produce a well-behaved, gradual stall onset, supporting consistent aerodynamic balance at the operating limit. Further simulation work should explore endplate geometry, Gurney flap sizing, and Reynolds number sensitivity to refine these recommendations.

  • High-Speed Circuits
    Use 0–5° for minimal drag. Efficiency peaks at 3.35 but downforce is limited.
  • Balanced / Medium-Speed
    10–20° is the recommended window offering efficient downforce generation.
  • Low-Speed / Maximum Grip
    30–45° for maximum downforce. Significant drag penalty must be budgeted.
  • Future Work
    Endplate geometry, Gurney flap sizing, Reynolds number sensitivity studies.
